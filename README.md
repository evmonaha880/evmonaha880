# Evan Monahan

**The bet: AI agents are the new shipping container.**

A shipping container is just corrugated steel. What was worth a trillion dollars was the system that reorganized around it — ships, ports, cranes, finance — once the world agreed on one universal box.

The agent is the next universal unit. It runs on any workflow the way the box rode any ship, and it absorbs the part of logistics that breaks people: the bottomless data, the signals, the thousand small calls a day. Standardize on that, and everything downstream rebuilds around it — the same way it did around the humble shipping container. 

### What I'm building

- **[latitude-longitude](https://github.com/evmonaha880/latitude-longitude)** — RL environments that grade the million-dollar GPU buy-and-hedge call under real chip-supply shocks. Not a forecast in a dashboard waiting to be interpreted — a model trained to hand an operator the actual call, the one they can go execute, and the risk they'd have missed. *Solo build · HUD Frontier RL hackathon.*
- **The Autonomous Container** — a Claude-powered agent that runs a shipping container as a self-governing economic unit. It senses its own state, reasons over its own P&L, and acts — reroutes, reprices, books its repairs, repositions itself — with no one at the controls. Today an empty box is dead weight: the industry burns tens of billions a year just moving empties around, and a container has no Carfax, no memory, no agency. This one runs itself. *In progress.*

### Why me

Supply chain isn't a pivot for me — it's the whole arc. It was my undergrad, it's been my obsession ever since, and I've worked nearly every layer of it, from the pick face to the C-suite. Three vantage points on one system:

- **Run it.** I started on the warehouse floor at Staples — running fulfillment, dispatching its delivery fleet — then launched same-day delivery there and built Darkstore's national hub network. I've designed operations from the dock up, not from a slide.
- **Advise it.** Then nearly seven years at Google, advising the C-suites of the world's largest retail and e-commerce brands through the most volatile stretch in supply chain since WWII — COVID, the Suez blockage, the Red Sea — on the calls that make or break an international business. I authored the *State of Supply Chain* thinking our partners read, and led the AI-partnership work that turned machine learning into operational decisions.
- **Build it.** Now I build the decision layer itself — RL environments that train procurement judgment under crisis, agents that take on logistics' heaviest data work and hand back the call. The tools above are the start.

I'm a Rochester, New York native (go Bills) — Hometown of George Eastman (Kodak), and not by accident the figure I keep circling back to. Eastman was an original supply-chain innovator: he didn't just invent roll film, he built the vertically integrated machine — manufacturing, distribution, a dealer network spanning the globe — that collapsed a darkroom craft into *"you press the button, we do the rest."* That's the move I'm chasing a century later: push the expertise into the system, and hand the operator the decision.
